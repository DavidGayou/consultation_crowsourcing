11. Should the provision of a hyperlink leading to a work or other subject matter
protected under copyright, either in general or under specific circumstances, be
subject to the authorisation of the rightholder? 

The  rights  of  the copyright  holder over  their  work should not extend  to  comprise
making a hyperlink.  Regular hyperlinks  should never be considered direct use of a
copyrighted work. Indeed, a regular hyperlink does not reproduce, transmit, nor really
make   available   in   any   way   a   work.   Rather,   hyperlinks   only   point   to   already
identifiable resources.  Other types of links, such as frames, however, might transmit
the works in some cases.

In   that   regard,   the   ruling  of   the   Court   of   Justice   of   European   Union   in  Svensson
C­466/12   is   worrisome.   Making   some   hyperlinking   practices   subject   to   the
authorisation   of   rightholders   only   complicates   their   use,   causes   chilling   effects   on
freedom of expression, sets up the unenforceable rules and leads to further alienation
of copyright law for the general public. Links to illegally communicated works should
be rather solved under theories of accessory liability or wrongful omission as they
account for flexible circumstances that might occur (absence of bad faith, duty of care,
etc.).

European   legislator   should   also   make   sure   that   even   non­harmonised   national
exclusive rights do not limit use of hyperlinks.

Suggestion: The right of communicated to the public should be therefore clarified to
exclude the use of regular hyperlinks from its scope regardless of the authorization of
the linked source. 

76.   In   particular,   is   the   current   legal   framework   clear   enough   to   allow   for
sufficient   involvement   of   intermediaries   (such   as   Internet   service   providers,
advertising brokers, payment service providers, domain name registrars, etc.) in
inhibiting   online   copyright   infringements   with   a   commercial   purpose?   If   not,
what measures would be useful to foster the cooperation of intermediaries?

Existing Union legal framework provides already for a very strong and flexible legal
basis for involvement of the Internet intermediaries – Art. 8(3) InfoSoc. This provision
provides  for injunctions against intermediaries, who got mixed up with third party
infringements,   but   have   not   committed   primary   or   secondary   infringements
themselves.   All   the   possible   administrative   measures   that   are   currently   considered
around the European Member states can be in fact constructed under this provision as
a  private law  remedy  (e.g. website blocking  injunctions, disconnecting  injunctions,
etc.). 

Existing experience from the Member states is, however, very diverse. Many countries
did   not   transpose   said   provision   in   the   way   that   was   intended   by   the   European
legislator.  And  even   countries   that   did   so,  face  rather   unusual  application   of   this
provision in cases, which not always take full account of the conflicting human rights
of affected parties, such as right to fair trail or right to private life (website blocking,
disconnecting, etc.). 
Suggestion: European legislator should first of all clarify all the existing rules. 

   •   Right   to   information,   for   instance,   should,  for   the   future,   regulate  with   a
       sufficient   clarity  the   entire  procedure   of  disclosure   of   identity   of   alleged
       infringers.  Such a disclosure  should be always subject to court oversight,  so
       that human rights requirements of  the  „quality of the law“, „proportionality“
       and interests of affected party are respected and safeguarded. 
   •   The legislator should also clarify  the  grounds for granting injunctions under
       Art. 8(3) InfoSoc  and make them autonomous  from the national law.  Some
       higher threshold that mere use of their services should be directly incorporated
       into the wording. 
   •   Also, more safeguards should be implement  directly in the provision  itself  in
       order to prevent abuse of these injunctions, which we currently  face  around
       European   jurisdictions   (see  more  HUSOVEC,   Martin.   Injunctions   Against
       Innocent Third Parties: Case of Website Blocking. 4 JIPITEC, 2, pp. 116­129,
       Max   Planck   Institute   for   Intellectual   Property   &   Competition   Law   Research
       Paper No. 13­14; http://ssrn.com/abstract=2257232).
