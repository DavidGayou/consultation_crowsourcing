       European	  Commission	  Public	  Consultation	  on	  the	  Review	  of	  
                         the	  EU	  Copyright	  Rules	  
	  
Foreword	  
	  
Copyright	  constitutes	  a	  significant	  tool,	  employed	  to	  incentivize,	  encourage	  and,	  
ultimately,	  protect	  creativity.	  Copyright	  has	  consistently	  contributed	  to	  culture	  
development,	  economic	  prosperity	  and	  social	  growth.	  To	  this	  end,	  a	  copyright	  
regime	  that	  is	  capable	  of	  offering	  the	  necessary	  incentives	  and	  one,	  which	  can	  
encourage	  creativity	  is	  key	  for	  Europe’s	  cultural,	  economic	  and	  social	  growth.	  
Moreover,	  a	  robust	  copyright	  regime	  can	  play	  a	  unique	  role	  in	  the	  wider	  Single	  
Market	  and	  contribute	  towards	  a	  digital	  economy	  framework	  that	  seeks	  to	  
encourage	  innovation	  and	  lead	  to	  economic	  development.	  	  
	  
Given	  the	  penetration	  of	  digital	  technologies	  in	  today’s	  societies,	  it	  is	  an	  opportune	  
time	  for	  discussions	  on	  the	  future	  of	  copyright	  in	  Europe	  to	  focus	  on	  how	  to	  create	  a	  
copyright	  regime	  that	  is	  aligned	  and	  takes	  into	  account	  the	  current	  realities	  of	  the	  
Internet	  and	  the	  way	  content	  is	  created.	  Nowadays,	  everyone	  can	  be	  a	  creator;	  the	  
Internet	  has	  equipped	  users	  with	  tools	  to	  produce	  and	  get	  access	  to	  content	  at	  
unprecedented	  levels.	  	  
	  
This	  reality	  poses	  many	  challenges	  and	  generates	  additional	  questions.	  On	  this	  issue,	  
the	  Internet	  Society	  is	  of	  the	  view	  that	  copyright	  can	  take	  advantage	  of	  the	  Internet	  
and	  its	  potential.	  New	  business	  models	  can	  encourage	  the	  broader	  dissemination	  
and	  distribution	  of	  intellectual	  protected	  material	  within	  Europe,	  can	  complement	  
existing	  ones	  and	  can	  create	  new	  or	  enhance	  existing	  partnerships	  based	  on	  multi-­‐
participatory	  models.	  We	  believe	  that	  for	  this	  to	  happen,	  effective	  use	  of	  intellectual	  
property	  tools	  and	  practices	  is	  required,	  which	  can	  help	  minimize	  the	  risks	  for	  
interested	  participants	  and	  play	  a	  major	  role	  in	  enhancing	  competitiveness	  of	  
technology-­‐based	  ideas.	  We	  also	  believe	  it	  further	  requires	  balanced	  and	  evidence-­‐
based	  intellectual	  property	  frameworks	  that	  are	  able	  to	  respect	  the	  limitations	  
instructed	  by	  the	  rule	  of	  law,	  operate	  under	  substantial	  due	  process	  requirements	  
and	  encourage	  non-­‐commercial	  use.	  	  
	  
The	  Internet	  Society’s	  input,	  in	  line	  with	  these	  general	  views,	  is	  given	  below.	  	  
	  
Introduction	  
	  
Similar	  to	  the	  decades-­‐long	  debate	  taking	  place	  at	  the	  global	  stage,	  copyright	  
discussions	  are	  focusing	  on	  how	  to	  align	  copyright	  law	  with	  the	  Internet	  and	  its	  
technologies.	  The	  central	  issue	  is	  whether	  copyright	  rules	  remain	  relevant	  in	  the	  age	  
of	  information	  and	  open	  standards	  networks.	  	  Questions	  (some	  of	  them	  raised	  also	  
in	  this	  consultation	  process)	  focus	  on	  whether	  copyright	  –	  under	  its	  current	  form	  –	  is	  
able	  to	  support	  innovation	  (as	  exemplified	  because	  of	  the	  Internet)	  or	  whether	  it	  can	  
encourage	  existing	  and	  new	  forms	  of	  creative	  expression.	  	  
	  
With	  the	  increasing	  involvement	  of	  more	  actors	  who	  both	  influence	  and	  get	  
influenced	  by	  copyright	  policy,	  it	  is	  important	  to	  have	  processes	  in	  place	  that	  are	  
inclusive	  of	  different	  and	  divergent	  voices.	  To	  this	  end,	  the	  Internet	  Society	  
appreciates	  the	  effort	  of	  the	  European	  Commission	  to	  solicit	  public	  responses	  and	  
encourage	  multistakeholder	  input	  on	  a	  range	  of	  issues	  relating	  to	  copyright.	  	  
	  
         A. Rights	  and	  the	  Functioning	  of	  the	  Single	  Market	  
	  
The	  Commission	  should	  use	  this	  consultation	  process	  to	  encourage	  regulatory	  
measures	  that	  will	  increase	  the	  cross-­‐border	  availability	  of	  content	  services	  in	  the	  
Single	  Market,	  while	  ensuring	  adequate	  protection	  for	  right	  holders	  and	  the	  public	  
interest.	  
	  
One	  of	  the	  most	  fundamental	  principles	  of	  intellectual	  property	  law	  –	  including	  
copyright	  –	  is	  that	  of	  territoriality.	  Notwithstanding	  the	  recent	  decision	  of	  the	  
Parliament	  to	  amend	  and	  adopt	  the	  Directive	  on	  “Collective	  management	  of	  
copyright	  and	  related	  rights	  and	  multi-­‐territorial	  licensing	  of	  rights	  in	  musical	  works	  
for	  online	  uses	  in	  the	  internal	  market”,	  European	  copyright	  law	  continues	  to	  impose	  
a	  framework	  of	  certain	  restrictions	  that	  operate	  to	  the	  detriment	  of	  EU’s	  freedoms	  
relating	  to	  the	  free	  movement	  of	  goods,	  capital,	  services	  and	  people.	  	  
	  
With	  the	  Internet,	  such	  territoriality’s	  implications	  come	  to	  the	  fore	  pressing	  for	  
immediate	  reconciliation	  of	  the	  global	  nature	  of	  the	  network	  with	  national	  copyright	  
laws.	  The	  Internet’s	  global	  nature	  and	  instantaneous	  capabilities	  for	  creating,	  
distributing	  and	  accessing	  content	  manifested	  more	  clearly	  the	  types	  of	  problems	  
the	  lack	  of	  having	  in	  place	  a	  common	  licensing	  structure	  may	  create.	  Users	  in	  Europe	  
should	  be	  able	  to	  access	  content	  in	  a	  synchronous	  and	  systematic	  way.	  
	  
An	  effective	  way	  to	  achieve	  this	  is	  for	  the	  Commission	  to	  ensure	  that	  copyrighted	  
material	  ‘travels’	  across	  the	  borders	  of	  all	  European	  Member	  States	  through	  a	  
licensing	  regime	  that	  is	  not	  bureaucratic,	  does	  not	  create	  unnecessary	  costs	  and	  –	  to	  
the	  extent	  possible	  –	  is	  compatible	  with	  the	  way	  the	  Internet	  works.	  The	  Internet	  
recognizes	  no	  physical	  boundaries	  by	  carrying	  data	  in	  a	  simultaneous	  way.	  The	  
physical	  place	  of	  where	  content	  is	  created	  becomes	  immaterial	  as	  data	  travels	  
systematically	  through	  Internet	  tubes.	  Removing	  the	  requirement	  for	  individual	  
licenses	  and	  moving	  towards	  a	  more	  integrated	  licensing	  scheme	  will	  facilitate	  
innovation	  and	  allow	  new	  business	  models	  to	  develop.	  	  A	  common	  license	  would	  
incentivize	  innovative	  and	  new	  business	  models	  within	  Europe,	  contributing	  to	  
Europe’s	  digital	  agenda	  and	  to	  a	  technology-­‐driven	  Union.	  	  
	  
In	  seeking	  to	  find	  the	  best	  solution,	  the	  Commission	  should	  consider	  how	  to	  better	  
marry	  technology	  with	  legal	  terminology.	  For	  instance,	  the	  recent	  interpretation	  of	  
the	  European	  Court	  of	  Justice1	  on	  the	  levels	  of	  control	  copyright	  owners	  should	  have	  
over	  linking	  and	  hyperlinking,	  successfully	  attempts	  to	  do	  this;	  we,	  therefore,	  invite

                           B. Limitations	  and	  Exceptions	  in	  the	  Single	  Market	  	  
	  
In	  light	  of	  the	  successful	  conclusion	  of	  the	  first	  international	  instrument	  of	  copyright	  
focusing	  on	  limitations	  and	  exceptions	  –	  the	  WIPO	  Treaty	  to	  Facilitate	  Access	  to	  
Published	  Works	  by	  Visually	  Impaired	  Persons	  and	  Persons	  with	  Print	  Disabilities	  –	  
the	  Commission	  has	  a	  unique	  opportunity	  to	  strengthen	  the	  culture	  of	  limitations	  
and	  exceptions,	  as	  they	  exist	  in	  Europe’s	  copyright	  law.	  	  
	  
Limitations	  and	  exceptions	  do	  not	  constitute	  complimentary	  provisions	  of	  copyright	  
law;	  rather,	  they	  are	  deeply	  ingrained	  in	  the	  philosophy,	  culture	  and	  core	  of	  the	  
copyright	  regime;	  they	  ensure	  inclusion	  and	  promote	  creative	  expression.	  It	  is	  
because	  of	  the	  affordability	  of	  limitations	  and	  exceptions	  that	  copyright	  is	  not	  an	  
absolute	  monopoly	  right,	  every	  use	  of	  copyrighted	  material	  is	  not	  subject	  to	  
permission	  and	  knowledge	  is	  not	  hindered.	  Moreover,	  without	  limitations	  and	  
exceptions	  the	  core	  function	  and	  role	  of	  certain	  institutions,	  in	  particular	  libraries,	  
would	  be	  placed	  in	  jeopardy.	  	  
	  
To	  this	  end,	  making	  limitations	  and	  exceptions	  optional	  tools	  of	  copyright	  endangers	  
the	  very	  foundation	  of	  what	  copyright	  is	  meant	  to	  preserve	  –	  the	  public	  interest.	  The	  
Commission	  could	  pave	  the	  way	  and	  encourage	  not	  only	  the	  mandatory	  adoption	  of	  
limitations	  and	  exceptions	  across	  all	  Member	  States	  but	  further	  facilitate	  their	  
integration	  into	  Europe’s	  single	  copyright	  law.	  	  
	  
The	  significance	  of	  limitations	  and	  exceptions	  is	  particular	  evident	  when	  it	  comes	  to	  
such	  issues	  like	  research.	  Whether	  through	  private	  agreements	  or	  through	  a	  more	  
institutionalized	  legal	  framework	  (e.g.	  Regulations,	  Directives,	  etc.)	  copyright	  law	  
ought	  to	  facilitate	  and	  enable	  research	  and	  research	  activities.	  Part	  of	  copyright’s	  
original	  and	  quintessential	  mandate	  is	  to	  assist	  the	  progress	  of	  science	  and	  
knowledge;	  in	  this	  sense,	  a	  significant	  element	  of	  copyright	  law	  resides	  “in	  a	  public	  
interest	  in	  having	  access	  to	  works	  for	  cultural,	  educational	  and	  scientific	  progress”.2

networked	  era	  –	  for	  researchers	  and	  students	  to	  be	  subjected	  to	  geo-­‐blocks.	  But,

copyright	  law	  has	  not	  done	  a	  good	  job	  in	  managing	  the	  expectations	  of	  users	  by	  
ensuring	  that	  such	  geo-­‐blocks	  are	  completely	  eliminated.	  To	  this	  end,	  there	  is	  a

Mass	  Digitization	  
	  
There	  lack	  of	  clarity	  regarding	  the	  parameters	  of	  authorial	  consent,	  which	  does	  not	  
only	  bear	  the	  cost	  of	  legal	  uncertainty,	  but	  it	  can	  also	  lead	  to	  new	  and	  not	  readily	  
transparent	  monopolies	  on	  information	  and	  knowledge.	  	  
	  
Mass	  digitization	  refers	  to	  the	  desire	  to	  make	  works	  available	  and	  a	  necessary	  
precondition	  of	  satisfying	  the	  commitment	  to	  the	  advancement	  of	  learning	  in	  the	  
digital	  age.	  Despite	  the	  fact	  that	  mass	  digitization	  has	  proven	  key	  for	  cultural	  
development	  and	  access	  to	  information	  and	  knowledge,	  it	  remains	  legally	  complex	  
and	  vague	  with	  an	  undetermined	  level	  of	  legitimacy.	  This	  is	  particularly	  evident	  
when	  it	  comes	  to	  access	  to	  and	  computation	  with	  works,	  which	  appear	  to	  raise	  more	  
issues	  compared	  to	  mere	  preservation	  of	  a	  work.	  This	  is	  due	  to	  the	  fact	  that	  they	  are	  
most	  easily	  exploited	  in	  a	  way	  that	  is	  unrelated	  to	  the	  public	  interest	  utility	  of	  
making	  works	  accessible.	  	  
	  
In	  this	  process,	  we	  would	  like	  to	  encourage	  the	  Commission	  to	  consider	  expanding	  
limitations	  and	  exceptions	  to	  include	  creativity	  expressed	  in	  the	  form	  of	  remixing	  
and	  mash-­‐ups,	  the	  status	  of	  which	  under	  the	  current	  copyright	  regime	  is	  highly	  
unsettled.	  There	  is	  an	  entire	  generation	  that	  is	  now	  being	  born	  and	  raised	  with	  the	  
Internet	  and	  the	  immense	  capabilities	  that	  it	  offers.	  It	  is	  essential	  that	  any	  copyright	  
framework	  incentivizes	  users	  to	  be	  creative	  and	  take	  full	  advantage	  of	  the	  
opportunities	  for	  innovation.	  	  
	  
Teaching	  and	  Research	  
	  
We	  would	  like	  to	  encourage	  the	  Commission	  to	  adopt	  a	  more	  harmonized	  approach

group	  of	  states	  that	  prides	  itself	  to	  be	  the	  place	  where	  content	  proliferated	  since	  
Ancient	  Greek	  times	  and	  continued	  to	  proliferate	  during	  the	  Renaissance and	  the

Disabilities	  
	  
The	  Internet	  Society	  followed	  closely	  the	  discussions	  and	  negotiations	  of	  the	  WIPO

Commission	  should	  encourage	  all	  Member	  States	  to	  ratify	  the	  Treaty	  as	  soon	  as

are	  eliminated.	  This	  is	  further	  compatible	  with	  the	  foundation	  of	  preserving	  the	  EU’s

Text	  and	  Data	  Mining	  	  
	  
The	  Internet	  Society	  agrees	  that	  text	  and	  data	  mining	  constitute	  inseparable	  parts	  of

behaviors,	  research	  or	  innovations	  related	  to	  the	  operation	  of	  the	  Internet.	  The	  
importance	  of	  standards	  in	  the	  Internet	  ecosystem	  is	  such	  that	  there	  are	  also	  many	  
isolated	  interconnected	  networks,	  which	  are	  not	  connected	  to	  the	  global	  Internet,	  
but	  use	  the	  Internet	  standards.	  The	  importance	  of	  preserving	  open	  standards	  was	  
also	  reflected	  in	  the	  recent	  EU	  Commission	  Communiqué	  on	  “Europe’s	  role	  in	  
shaping	  the	  future	  of	  Internet	  Governance”,	  where	  the	  Commission	  encouraged	  
“support	  [of]	  the	  implementation	  of	  open	  standards	  by	  the	  European	  Internet	  
industry	  and	  the	  involvement	  of	  the	  European	  Internet	  industry	  in	  the	  development	  of	  
open	  Internet	  standards”.8	  
	  
In	  this	  environment,	  the	  Internet	  Society	  believes	  that	  open	  standards,	  innovation	  
and	  intellectual	  property	  can	  complement	  each	  other.	  We	  believe	  that	  copyright	  can	  
take	  advantage	  of	  the	  Internet’s	  openness,	  the	  same	  way	  users	  have	  taken	  
advantage	  of	  it	  to	  create,	  remix	  and	  generally	  generate	  content.	  Unfortunately,	  
under	  its	  current	  state,	  copyright	  appears	  to	  be	  at	  odds	  with	  the	  Internet;	  for	  
example,	  websites	  often	  abuse	  the	  Internet’s	  openness	  by	  employing	  automated	  
copyright	  filters	  and	  tools	  that	  seek	  to	  filter	  and/or	  block	  copyrighted	  content	  or	  
parts	  of	  it,	  which	  ultimately	  might	  stifle	  the	  creativity	  that	  is	  currently	  exemplified	  
through	  remixing.	  Another	  example	  is	  the	  discussions	  regarding	  the	  role	  of	  
intermediary	  actors	  in	  preserving	  copyright.	  The	  Internet	  Society	  always	  argued	  
about	  the	  need	  to	  preserve	  the	  clear	  roles	  and	  responsibilities	  of	  each	  of	  the	  actors	  
in	  the	  Internet	  ecosystem	  and	  intermediaries	  should	  not	  be	  asked	  to	  be	  in	  charge	  of	  
substantive	  determinations;	  rather,	  the	  Commission	  should	  continue	  to	  encourage	  
transparency	  and	  cooperation	  in	  good	  faith.	  
	  
History	  is	  replete	  with	  examples	  of	  how	  innovation	  and	  creativity	  depend	  on	  the	  
exchange	  and	  the	  borrowing	  of	  ideas.	  One	  of	  the	  most	  noteworthy	  examples	  is	  
Henry	  Ford,	  who	  echoed	  the	  story	  of	  Marconi	  and	  the	  invention	  of	  the	  radio,	  stating	  
at	  the	  time:	  “I	  invented	  nothing	  new.	  I	  simply	  assembled	  the	  discoveries	  of	  other	  men	  
behind	  whom	  were	  centuries	  of	  work.	  Progress	  happens	  when	  all	  the	  factors	  that	  
make	  for	  it	  are	  ready	  and	  then	  it	  is	  inevitable”.	  
	  
To	  this	  end,	  it	  becomes	  crucial	  that	  copyright	  law	  makes	  room	  for	  new	  Internet	  
business	  models	  that	  can	  encourage	  and	  support	  a	  space	  for	  creativity	  and	  can	  
	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  	  
8	  Communication	  from	  the	  Commission	  to	  the	  European	  Parliament,	  the	  Council,	  the	  European	  
Economic	  and	  Social	  Committee	  and	  the	  Committee	  of	  the	  Regions,	  Internet	  Policy	  and	  Governance,	  
“Europe’s	  role	  in	  shaping	  the	  future	  of	  Internet	  Governance”,	  http://eur-­‐
lex.europa.eu/LexUriServ/LexUriServ.do?uri=COM:2014:0072:FIN:EN:PDF	  	  
create	  new	  or	  enhance	  existing	  partnerships	  based	  on	  multi-­‐participatory	  models.	  
For	  this	  to	  occur,	  however,	  it	  requires	  effective	  use	  of	  copyright	  tools	  and	  practices	  
that	  can	  help	  minimize	  the	  risks	  for	  interested	  participants	  and	  play	  a	  major	  role	  in	  
enhancing	  competitiveness	  of	  technology-­‐based	  ideas.	  It	  further	  requires	  a	  balanced	  
copyright	  framework	  that	  is	  able	  to	  respect	  the	  limitations	  instructed	  by	  
proportionality	  and	  the	  rule	  of	  law	  and	  operates	  under	  substantial	  due	  process	  
requirements.	  
	  
In	  2013,	  the	  Internet	  Society	  reflecting	  on	  intellectual	  property	  issues	  came	  to	  the	  
following	  conclusion,	  pursuant	  to	  a	  broad	  consultative	  process:	  when	  we	  talk	  about	  
a	  balanced	  intellectual	  property	  framework,	  we	  should,	  additionally,	  bear	  in	  mind	  a	  
structure	  that,	  amongst	  others,	  fosters	  competitive	  markets	  and	  enables	  new	  actors	  
to	  enter.	  Copyright	  constitutes	  essentially	  a	  government	  grant	  of	  a	  costly	  private	  
monopoly	  over	  ideas;	  this	  monopoly	  is	  not	  meant	  to	  encourage	  restrictive	  
structures.	  Standing	  alone,	  copyright	  may	  be	  able	  to	  exclude	  others,	  but	  it	  should	  
not	  be	  used	  to	  forestall	  the	  introduction	  of	  innovative	  and	  creative	  ideas.9
